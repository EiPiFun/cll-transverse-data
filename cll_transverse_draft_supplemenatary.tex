\subsection*{Models and relaxations of cellulose nanocrystals Type I}\indent
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{\protect\numberline{}Models and relaxations of cellulose nanocrystals Type I}\indent

Referring to the transverse crystal lattice dimension data (a=0.778~nm, b=0.820~nm, c=1.038~nm) and the cellulose chain residue orientation (tg or gt type), cellulose nanocrystals Type I models were constructed (Figure \ref{fig:cellulose_nanocrystal_structure_and_models}).
In the cellulose nanocrystals Type I, all the chains are composed of tg type residues.
According to the hydrogen bonding pattern, the cellulose nanocrystals Type I has a specific orthogonal structure with three characteristic directions, along, perpendicular to, and slant to the hydrogen bonding layers.

For the slant model with frictional sliding phenomenon, 144 chains were used, while the brittle vertical and horizontal models contained 72 chains, respectively.
The structures after relaxation, and the RMSD (root mean square deviation) curves of models were shown in Figure \ref{fig:cellulose_nanocrystal_relaxations}.
These data exhibit stability of the cellulose nanocrystals Type I models.

\subsection*{Hydrogen bond number and potential energy during stretches of the Type I}\indent
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{\protect\numberline{}Hydrogen bond number and potential energy during stretches of the Type I}\indent

The dominant role of hydrogen bonds and Coulomb interactions should be further elucidated, and the number of hydrogen bonds, the Coulomb, and van der Waals potential energy during stretches, were all shown in Figure \ref{fig:cellulose_nanocrystal_stretch_hydrogen_bond_number_and_energy}.
These data were all delta, and relative to their corresponding minimum values, which is also setting the zero potential energy surface effectively.
For the vertical model with highest strength, it is the only case where Coulomb potential increased, emphasizing the particular dominant role of hydrogen bonds in this characteristic direction.
And the most reduction of hydrogen bond number also supports this, while the slant model recovered hydrogen bond number partially after the frictional sliding.
However, the van der Waals potential energy all increased after stretches, elucidating the general resisting ability of van der Waals interaction against external loads.

Size dependency of cellulose nanocrystals Type II would also be quantitatively explained by the delta potential energy density, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:cellulose_nanocrystal_ii_delta_energy_density_with_size}.
The delta potential energy density was also defined as the ratio of delta potential energy, and the volume before stretches.
With a increasing size, the absolute value of delta potential energy density gradually decreased, thus the structures were weaker to resist stretches.
This could also be explained from the morphological perspective, as the overall volume of the varied with the length at the second order, while the fractured interface area only increased at a lower order (approximately linearly).

    As the size of the nanocrystal increased, the delta potential energy density during stretches gradually decreased, particularly for the van der Waals interaction resisting tension.
    From the perspective of structure, larger nanocrystals have a stronger ability to maintain themselves.

\subsection*{Transverse arrangement patterns of the Type II}\indent

The symmetric pattern was inspired by twin structure and the cross pattern was a further symmetric one.
To form a model, 4 unit blocks were arranged, where unit block size included 5$\times$8, 7$\times$12, and 10$\times$16 rows and columns.
As shown in the figures, only the symmetric patten models under vertical stretches could present high ductility and strength.
The weak interfaces in the symmetric under horizontal stretches and cross pattern, further confirmed the performance dependency of crystal structures and periodicity.
For the symmetric pattern under vertical stretches, it is also obvious that their ductility and toughness depended on the size of unit blocks.
